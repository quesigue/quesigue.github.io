Uruguay

Julka&Lulka

Posted on April 26, 2013

 

For the last few days we were discovering the coast of Uruguay. We started in Colonia, where we’ve come by boat from Buenos Aires. Colonia was a refuge of peace and calmness after crowdy and noisy Baires, how the people call the capital of Argentina.. On the streets there was almost no one, only a few tourists walking around the old town. Beautiful colorful houses (summer houses rather than year-round), a small harbor with boats, tiny streets with benches decorated with ceramic tiles. We spent there only 2-3 hours walking, and from there we took a bus to the capital of Uruguay, Montevideo ie.

 

Typical street in Colonia

 

We had a little trouble with contacting our next host (our phone didn’t work, so we had to look for access to the Internet). Once we managed to get to the right apartment, it was not Mariano’s, with who we wrote, but his friend Santiago’s. Santiago was very hospitable – he gave us his room to sleep in, he agreed to keep our big backpacks at his place while we went to the coast of Uruguay, and told us  what to see in Montevideo. On the same day we met Mariano himself. His sense of humor made it difficult  for us to determine whether he is trustworthy. But that was just the first impression

 

The view from Santiago’s flat

We were sightseeing in Montevideo only for one day because boys were going to Punta del Este the next day and so we decided to go with them. They often go there for the weekends and this weekend was to be the last warm one before the upcoming winter. Moreover, the route recommended for us by Santiago included only the promenade by the sea and a few streets in the old town. Other areas on the map were marked by crosses (X-es) and that meant we shouldn’t go there. The promenade stretches the length of the whole Montevideo, so the people of each district gather there after work and do many activities like jogging, riding a bike, skating etc. We regretted , that we do not have a bike (28 degrees Celsius and the lack of shade for several kilometers can be tiring).

 

The promenade in Montevideo.

 

 

The Old Town was quite empty. It wasn’t big at all, just several streets with pubs with steak next to  abandoned old houses with graffiti on them. Miel , about whom we wrote in the previous post, drew our attention to the artistry of the local graffiti artists. She admitted that when she travels she always pays attention to the steet art because they can say a lot about the habitants and the city itself.

 

 

Street art in Montevideo.

 

The same day, late in the evening, we were already in the Mariano’s family countryside home, near Punta del Este and La Barra. In the morning we saw how beautiful the house is, with its glassy walls and an amazing view. And finally it was time for the beach and ocean! Over the next three days we visited various beaches (La Barra, La Pedrera, La Paloma) we were swimming, surfing, running and, OK., a little bit of frying at the beach too. A very enjoyable holiday.

 

Mariano´s family cottage house

 

 

 

On Sunday, the guys had already come back to Montevideo, while we moved on to Punta del Diablo. We knew we wanted to stay in that very small town, because it was recommended to us by a few friends back in Poland. As soon as we got out of the bus, we asked a police officer  where is an open hostel here somewhere. Without a moment of hesitation he told us to go to El Diablo Tranquillo, and so that was the same place where two years earlier our good friend Franek stopped. 12.90 USD per night, including breakfast, a joint 10-person dorm, where besides us there was one girl from Brazil and one boy from England. As the hostel was also a bar, every night people were coming down there and talked until late. We met some people from England, New Zealand, Australia, Brazil, Argentina, Texas … and only one Uruguayan who was an employee of the hostel. Others who ran the hostel used to be turists who during their stay decided to prolong it and instead of a week or two, there are here for  few months now.

 

Our hostel, El Diablo Tranquillo.

 

Although Punta del Diablo is a very small town, it offers a lot of attractions. Surfing, horse riding, scooters, a huge nature reserve, “black lake”, watching the sea lions (in nearby Cabo Polonia). We’ve decided to opt for a long walk (a bit TOO long actually) along the beach to the Santa Teresa reserve,  which in total had about 10 kms and took us six hours. However, the views and the smell of plants were amazing. Unfortunately, we had a less pleasant encounter with the animals .. We saw the first hairy, ugly spider (it was probably a real TARANTULA)! In the evening, we were so tired that we didn’t even have the strength to sit and talk in a common room – we only cooked our first self-made dinner (pasta with some delicious vegetables ) and tried the local wine from the box, which turned out not so bad. Even such a great news from Magda and Diego that they would willingly accomodate us on their ranch in Esquina  (in Argentina) and offer us a two-day horse-back trip, did not give us the strength to continue the evening. It was not until the morning we were able to fully enjoy that fact.

 

In Santa Teresa.

Ugly tarantula.

SiS scarf can also protect you from the sun

 

 

It is our last day in Uruguay today – we’re heading back to Santiago’s where we’d left our backpacks  and at 6.30 in the morning we have a bus  to Iguazu. It is going to be a very exhausting and long journey. We plan to see the famous waterfalls on Friday, and on Sunday we have to be in Esquina.
